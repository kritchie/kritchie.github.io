    How to read a paper #

    Note: This is my own digest of [1] and is kept as a reference for future readings.

    Problem: The author presents the problem of students having difficulty reading research papers.

    Contribution: The author presents the “three-pass” approach.

    Three-pass approach #

    Pass 1 #

    Do the following:

    1. Read title, abstract and introduction.
    2. Read section and sub-sections headings.
    3. Read conclusion.

    To understand the “five-Cs”:

    1. Category. What type of paper is this?
    2. Context. Which other papers is it related to? Which theoretical bases were used to analyze the problem?
    3. Correctness: Do the assumptions appear to be valid?
    4. Contributions: What are the paperâ€™s main contributions?
    5. Clarity: Is the paper well written?

    Pass 2 #

    Do the following:

    1. Look at figures, diagrams and illustrations.
    2. Mark relevant unread references for further reading.

    To understand the main idea of the paper. After which you can:

    1. Set paper aside.
    2. Read background material first and come back later.
    3. Go to pass 3.

    Pass 3 #

    […] attempt to virtually re-implement the paper […] challenge every assumption in every statement.

    This is where you get the most insights into the innovations of the paper.

    You should be able to reconstruct the paper and identify its strengths and weaknesses.

    On literature surveys… #

    If you find a literature survey, use it instead to replace the pass 1 on the included papers.

    Find key papers and key authors in the references.

    Checklist for note taking #

    The following was greatly inspired from [2].

    • Extract full citation
    • Extract key words
    • Identify general subject and category
    • Identify the research question (the problem)
    • Identify the contributions
    • Identify the dataset (if any)
    • Identify the methodology used by the authors
    • Explain the innovations or techniques used
    • Explain how it relates to other work in the field
    • Extract important figures / tables / diagrams

    Reference(s) #

    [1] Keshav, S. (2007). How to read a paper. ACM SIGCOMM Computer Communication Review, 37(3), 83-84.

    [2] Purugganan, M., & Hewitt, J. (2004). How to read a scientific article. Rice University.
